I Built IC Because No One Built It for Me

Leadership & Growth / Dec 2025

The things that break you are the same things that build you. That's why I created a space for others to do both.

What New York Taught Me

I did judo from the age of 9 to 17. I never liked sports. It was my father's coercion. Instead, he never asked me about my grades. The education was that learning through failure and competition is more important than studying just to get into a good company. I didn't know what it meant at the time.

At 17, I came to New York. I didn't even know where America was, and I had never been outside my hometown. The language was different, I didn't really know the alphabet. I had never been a socially underprivileged person because of sports in Korea, but here it was different. For the first time, I felt I was a socially underprivileged person.

I held on. But as time went on, I got depressed, and I wanted to give up everything. My experience is not unusual. Studies show that about 35% of international students in the U.S. experience depression. I was one of them.

But I drew on what I had learned through sports. There was no reason. I just kept going until it worked. I held on like that.

So I Built IC

It was such a waste to have this experience alone. Because I knew that people become stronger only when they face failures and challenges firsthand.

I created Infinite Challenge at Penn State. The name comes from Muhan Dojeon, a beloved Korean variety show. It was a show where ordinary people took on bold challenges and found hope. I felt like that was me.

At IC, we did skydiving, marathons, drone racing, scuba diving, and even app development. It wasn't a group for extreme sports. My goal was to give people an experience they had never done before and push their limits. Now IC has gone beyond a school club to become a startup.

I don't have anything in particular. But I've come this far. That's why IC exists.

The IC team — people who chose to face their fears and grow together
