Youth Sports Is Booming — but Marketing’s Real Job Isn’t Ads.

In youth sports, the real win isn’t a clever ad — it’s getting one more kid to show up to practice. Marketing’s job here is participation, not impressions.

The numbers are moving fast

Youth sports is growing, not shrinking. According to L.E.K. Consulting, team-sport participation among kids ages 6 to 17 rose 9% year over year in 2024.

Families are spending more, too. Average annual spend per young athlete climbed from about $700 in 2019 to roughly $1,000 in 2024 — and nearly a third of families take on debt to keep their kids playing.

What that means for marketers

When parents stretch their budgets like that, they aren’t waiting to be sold. The bottleneck isn’t awareness — it’s giving people a clear, repeated reason to commit and come back.

My research work with SpoAny, a Korean fitness brand, taught me the same lesson. Marketing wasn’t about flashy promos; it was about removing friction and building real reasons to show up.

A Red Bull-style youth academy works the same way. The goal isn’t impressions — it’s participation: registrations, attendance, kids who stay through the season. Count those, and the ads start to take care of themselves.
